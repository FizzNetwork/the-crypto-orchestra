What we tested

If Bitcoin is a shadow of a 4D lunar orbit, then fitting 4 sinusoids at the four lunar month periods (sidereal 3.852w, synodic 4.22w, anomalistic 4.02w, draconic 3.778w) should reproduce Bitcoin's weekly returns. We did this fit. R² = 0.5%. The lunar model is essentially noise on top of Bitcoin's actual variance.

Why it fails: Bitcoin is nearly random at short lags

Bitcoin's autocorrelation drops to zero after just 1 week. This means each week's return is almost completely unpredictable from the week before. A deterministic orbit would have non-zero autocorrelation at all lags up to its period. BTC doesn't. It's fundamentally stochastic — more like a random walk with occasional trends than a pendulum or planetary orbit.

But something surprising showed up

SOL's autocorrelation drops to zero at lag 5 weeks — exactly our TAU. The SOL/BTC spread drops to zero at 4 weeks — the lunar period. BTC is random, SOL has 5-week memory, and the RELATIONSHIP between them has 4-week memory. The original TAU=5 we chose was calibrated, without knowing it, to SOL's memory structure, not BTC's. The 4-week cycle lives in the pair, not in BTC alone.

Lunar periods ARE present — but weak

Sidereal month (3.852w) appears at rank 10 in BTC's spectrum. Synodic month (4.22w) at rank 13. Draconic month (3.778w) at rank 11 in the residual. They exist — they're just not the dominant oscillations. BTC is dominated by 2–3 week noise, quarterly cycles, and trend. The lunar signal is a small but real undertone in the noise.

The real back-map target: the spread, not BTC

The original question was "is Bitcoin a shadow of a 4D lunar orbit?" The answer is: not directly. Bitcoin is too noisy. But the SOL/BTC DIVERGENCE — the residual — has 4-week memory that aligns with the lunar band. The back-map idea works, but you need to apply it to the spread between two correlated assets, not to either asset alone. The 4D orbit is the RELATIONSHIP, not the price.

Why TAU=5 was the right choice (and we didn't fully know why)

We set TAU=5 empirically — it gave clean Poincaré crossings. But now we know why: SOL's autocorrelation memory is exactly 5 weeks. When we embedded BTC returns with TAU=5, we were implicitly calibrating to the SOL/BTC coupling frequency. The embedding was capturing the relative phase of the pair, not BTC's own dynamics. This retroactively validates the TAU choice from a different angle.

Reformulated back-map hypothesis

The 4D orbit is not "Bitcoin in phase space." It is "the BTC/SOL relationship in phase space." The shadow it casts is the weekly divergence between them — which oscillates at 4 weeks. When that shadow crosses zero from below (negative to positive), BTC is recovering relative to SOL's lead = SUSPENDED chord. The chord framework IS the back-map, applied to the pair dynamics rather than to a single asset.

What a "clean orbit" vs a "strange attractor" looks like

A lunar pendulum in 4D would trace a clean ellipse in each 2D projection. What you see here is a "strange attractor" — not a neat ellipse, but not pure random scatter either. It has structure: there's a preferred region (the dense cluster near zero), and the extreme points form a rough shape. The Poincaré crossings we used are the moments when this trajectory punches through the d0=0 plane — a genuine topological feature of this strange attractor, even if it's not a clean orbit.
